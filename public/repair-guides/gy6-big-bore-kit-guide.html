GY6 Big Bore Kit — Everything You Need to Know

📌 Original sources: GY6 Big Bore Kits — Everything You Need to Know — Rolling Wrench & GY6 Big Bore Kit Prep — Buggy Depot

A big bore kit is the most impactful single upgrade you can make to a GY6 engine. By fitting a larger cylinder and matched piston, displacement increases — bringing noticeably improved acceleration and higher top speed without requiring exotic parts. This guide covers what big bore kits are, how to choose the right one, and how to install it correctly.

What Is a Big Bore Kit?

A big bore kit replaces the stock cylinder, piston, piston rings, and sometimes the cylinder head with larger-bore equivalents. The crankcase, crankshaft, and valve train typically remain stock. The displacement increase comes entirely from the wider bore — stroke length stays the same.

On a standard GY6 150cc engine (157QMJ, 57 mm bore), a 171cc upgrade uses a 61 mm bore cylinder. This 4 mm bore increase raises displacement from 150 cc to approximately 171 cc — a gain of about 14 percent.

Available Upgrades for GY6 Engines

Engine Stock Bore Big Bore Option New Displacement Machining Required?
GY6 50cc (139QMB) 39 mm 47 mm kit (70cc) ~70 cc No — drop-in on most B-Block cases
GY6 150cc (157QMJ) 57 mm 61 mm kit (171cc) ~171 cc No — fits stock A-Block cases
GY6 150cc (157QMJ) 57 mm 63 mm kit (180cc) ~180 cc Yes — case boring required
⚠️ Beware of "No Machining Required" 63 mm Kits: Some vendors sell 63 mm kits claiming no case machining is needed. Experienced builders strongly advise against these — the cylinder wall is too thin for long-term reliability at that bore size without proper case preparation. If you want 180cc, have the cases professionally bored.

What to Buy — Kit Components

A reputable big bore kit should include: a new cylinder (barrel), piston with circlips, piston rings, a base gasket, and a head gasket. Some kits include a matching cylinder head; others require you to reuse or source a head separately.

Quality varies enormously. Key markers of a good kit:

  • Cylinder bore is honed (cross-hatch pattern visible inside) — not just drilled
  • Piston rings fit the ring grooves precisely — no side-play when pushed down
  • Ring end-gaps are within specification — check with feeler gauges (see below)
  • Base gasket is the correct thickness for your head gasket combination

Tools Required

  • Socket set and combination spanners (8, 10, 12, 14, 17 mm)
  • Torque wrench (capable of 10–50 Nm)
  • Feeler gauges
  • Piston ring compressor (or careful hands)
  • Gasket scraper / razor blade
  • Engine oil and assembly lube
  • Thread-locking compound (Loctite 243 or equivalent)
  • Camera or phone — photograph everything before disassembly

Installation Procedure

Step 1: Prepare the Engine

  1. Allow the engine to cool completely. Drain the engine oil.
  2. Remove the seat, bodywork panels, and air filter assembly to gain access to the engine.
  3. Disconnect the spark plug cap. Remove the spark plug.
  4. For easier access, consider removing the engine from the frame (disconnect the carburetor, exhaust, wiring loom, rear shock mount, and engine hanger bolt).

Step 2: Remove the Cylinder Head and Cylinder

  1. Remove the valve cover (cam cap). Photograph the cam chain routing before disassembly.
  2. Rotate the engine to Top Dead Centre (TDC) on the compression stroke — both valves closed, timing mark aligned.
  3. Remove the cam chain tensioner bolt and slide the tensioner out.
  4. Undo the camshaft bearing cap (two bolts). Lift out the camshaft carefully, keeping the cam chain loop over the sprocket — secure it with a zip tie through the chain to prevent it dropping into the case.
  5. Undo the four cylinder head nuts in a cross pattern. Lift the head off — it may need gentle tapping with a rubber mallet. Do not use a metal hammer directly on the head.
  6. Lift the cylinder off the studs. The piston will be exposed. Place a clean rag over the crankcase mouth immediately to prevent debris entering.

Step 3: Remove the Old Piston

  1. Note which way the piston arrow or notch faces (it must point toward the exhaust side on reassembly).
  2. Use a small screwdriver or circlip pliers to remove the piston pin circlips from both sides of the piston. Keep these — they are reusable if undamaged.
  3. Push the piston pin out by hand or with a dowel. The piston will slide off the connecting rod.

Step 4: Check and Prepare the New Components

  1. Inspect the new cylinder bore — it should be smooth with a visible cross-hatch honing pattern.
  2. Check piston ring end-gaps by placing each ring individually inside the new cylinder bore and measuring the gap with feeler gauges. Standard acceptable range: 0.15–0.40 mm. Replace rings if gaps are outside this range.
  3. Lubricate the cylinder bore, piston, and piston pin with fresh engine oil.
  4. Install the piston rings onto the new piston — top ring, second ring, then oil scraper ring. Stagger the ring gaps 120° apart around the piston to minimise blow-by.

Step 5: Install the New Piston and Cylinder

  1. Fit the new piston onto the connecting rod with the arrow or "EX" marking facing the exhaust port. Insert the piston pin and reinstall the circlips — ensure both circlips are fully seated in their grooves.
  2. Clean the crankcase mating surface and cylinder base — remove all old gasket material.
  3. Place the new base gasket on the crankcase studs (dry — no sealant).
  4. Oil the cylinder bore again. Compress the piston rings using a ring compressor or your thumbs. Carefully lower the new cylinder over the piston, keeping the cam chain guided through the chain tunnel.
  5. Seat the cylinder fully on the base gasket.

Step 6: Reassemble the Top End

  1. Install the new head gasket. Lower the cylinder head into place over the studs.
  2. Fit the head nuts finger-tight, then torque in a cross pattern: 10–12 Nm for M6 studs. Do not overtighten — the aluminium will strip.
  3. Reinstall the camshaft, aligning the timing marks on the cam sprocket with the marks on the cylinder head.
  4. Reinstall the cam chain tensioner. Reinstall the valve cover.
  5. Reinstall the spark plug: 10–14 Nm.

Step 7: Re-jet the Carburetor

A larger engine displaces more air per intake stroke. The stock main jet (typically 95–105 for a 150cc GY6) will run lean with a 171cc cylinder. Fit a larger main jet — start 5–10 sizes up from stock (e.g., jet #108 for a 61 mm / 171cc build). Test and adjust until the spark plug reads a light tan colour after a full-throttle run.

💡 Re-torque After First Heat Cycle: After the engine has warmed up to operating temperature for the first time and cooled back down, re-torque the cylinder head nuts. Aluminium expands and contracts significantly with temperature, which can cause head gasket settling. This step prevents head gasket failure in the first few hundred kilometres.

Common Mistakes

  • Forgetting to re-jet: Running lean after a displacement increase will burn the piston. Always re-jet.
  • Installing rings with gaps aligned: Piston rings must have staggered gaps. Aligned gaps cause excessive blow-by and oil consumption.
  • Overtorquing the head: The aluminium head strips very easily. Use a torque wrench — don't rely on feel.
  • Dropping the cam chain: Secure it with a zip tie before removing the camshaft. Retrieving a dropped cam chain can mean splitting the cases.
  • Skipping the break-in period: For the first 200–300 km after a big bore install, avoid full throttle. Vary engine speed to allow the rings to bed in properly.

Expected Performance Gains

A 150cc → 171cc big bore conversion on a GY6 scooter typically delivers: 10–15% more torque at low-to-mid RPM, noticeably stronger acceleration from a standstill, and a modest top speed increase (2–5 km/h in most cases). The biggest benefit is felt in the mid-range — the scooter pulls harder up hills and when two-up riding.

For further gains, the big bore upgrade pairs well with a performance exhaust, upgraded variator rollers matched to the new power delivery, and — if running carburetion — a matching performance air filter and jet kit.
