GY6 Carburetor Jetting & Rejetting Guide

Jetting refers to selecting the correct size of the brass main jet and pilot jet inside the carburetor to achieve the right air-to-fuel ratio for your engine's conditions. A stock GY6 scooter is jetted conservatively from the factory — usually slightly lean — which can cause hesitation, flat spots, or overheating. If you have added an aftermarket exhaust, performance air filter, or high-compression piston, rejetting is essential.

Understanding the Throttle Zones

The GY6 slide-type carburetor (PD-series or CVK) controls the mixture across the throttle range via different circuits. Knowing which circuit governs each zone tells you which component to adjust:

  • 0–¼ throttle (idle and off-idle): Governed by the pilot jet and the air/fuel mixture screw. Symptoms of a lean pilot: stumbling, stalling at idle, engine hunts.
  • ¼–¾ throttle (mid-range): Governed by the needle position and its clip groove. A needle that is too lean causes hesitation when rolling on from partial throttle.
  • ¾–full throttle (wide-open): Governed by the main jet size. A lean main jet causes flat top-end power and possible overheating at sustained full throttle.

Jetting for Modifications

Performance Air Filter

A high-flow air filter (K&N style pod filter or similar) allows more air through the carburetor. This leans the mixture and requires a main jet increase. A typical starting point is to increase the main jet by 5–8 sizes (e.g., from #98 to #105 on a 150cc engine) and adjust from there using the spark plug color method.

Performance Exhaust

An aftermarket exhaust that reduces back-pressure also leans the mixture, especially in the mid-to-high throttle range. Increase the main jet by 3–5 sizes as a starting point, and raise the needle one clip position (move the clip from the 3rd to the 2nd groove from the top).

Altitude Correction

At higher altitudes, air is thinner, so the stock jetting becomes progressively richer as altitude increases. A rough rule of thumb is to decrease the main jet by 3 sizes per 1,000 m of altitude above sea level.

How to Change a Main Jet

  1. Ensure the engine is cold. Turn the fuel tap off.
  2. Remove the float bowl (see the Deep-Cleaning Guide for step-by-step bowl removal).
  3. Unscrew the main jet with a flat-blade screwdriver. Note the existing size stamped on the jet body.
  4. Thread in the new jet finger-tight, then snug with the screwdriver. Do not overtighten.
  5. Refit the float bowl, turn the fuel on, and start the engine.
  6. After a warm-up run, remove the spark plug and read its color. A correct mixture gives a tan to light brown plug tip. Black = too rich; white/grey = too lean.
  7. Adjust one jet size at a time until the plug color is correct.
⚠️ Always clean the carburetor thoroughly before rejetting. A partially blocked jet will give false lean symptoms regardless of jet size. See the Deep-Cleaning Guide first.
💡 Tip: Jets are typically sized in increments of 2–3 (e.g., #95, #98, #100, #102, #105). Buy a jet kit covering a range of ±15 from your stock size so you can tune without repeated ordering.

Where to Buy GY6 Jets
