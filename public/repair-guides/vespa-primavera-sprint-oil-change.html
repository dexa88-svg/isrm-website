Vespa Primavera & Sprint 125–150 Oil Change Guide

📌 Primary sources: DIY Vespa oil change — Primavera / GTS / Sprint (YouTube) and DIY Vespa Sprint 150 and Primavera 150 1st Oil Change (YouTube)

Regular engine oil changes are the single most important maintenance task for any petrol scooter. The Vespa Primavera (2013–present) and Sprint (2014–present) share the same Piaggio 3-valve iGet engine family in 125 cc and 150 cc displacements. Both models use fuel injection and require an oil change every 3,000 km (approximately 1,900 miles) or at least once a year — whichever comes first.

This guide covers all current iGet-engine Primavera and Sprint variants: Primavera 125, Primavera 150, Sprint 125, Sprint 150, and their S, Sport, and Racing Sixties trim levels. The procedure is identical for all.

Difficulty & Time

Difficulty: Beginner-friendly. No specialist tools required beyond a 17 mm spanner and a torque wrench. Time: 20–30 minutes including warm-up and clean-up.

Step-by-Step Procedure

Step 1 — Warm the Engine

  1. Start the scooter and let it idle for 3–5 minutes. Warm oil flows freely and carries more contaminants out of the engine than cold oil does.
  2. Shut the engine off and wait 2–3 minutes so the oil settles and cools slightly — you want warm, not scalding.

Step 2 — Position and Prepare

  1. Place the scooter on its centre stand on a level surface.
  2. Place the drain pan below and slightly forward of the engine — the oil drain plug is on the underside-front of the engine casing, facing toward the front wheel.
  3. Remove the front under-panel if fitted (two or three Phillips screws) to give clear access to the drain plug area. Some models allow access without removing any panels.

Step 3 — Remove the Filler Cap

  1. Open the seat and locate the oil filler cap on the top of the engine. It has a dipstick attached and is marked with an oil-can symbol.
  2. Twist anti-clockwise and pull straight up to remove. Set aside on a clean surface.
  3. Opening the filler cap first creates a small air vent that helps oil drain faster.

Step 4 — Drain the Old Oil

  1. Apply the 17 mm socket to the drain plug. Turn anti-clockwise (viewed from below) to loosen. Once loose, use your fingers to remove — support the plug so it does not drop into the drain pan.
  2. Allow the engine to drain fully — typically 3–5 minutes. Tilt the scooter slightly toward the drain side if oil flow slows.
  3. Inspect the old oil on a white cloth: black, gritty oil indicates normal combustion by-products; metallic flakes require further engine investigation before refilling.
  4. Clean the drain plug threads with a rag. Discard the old copper sealing washer.

Step 5 — Refit the Drain Plug

  1. Fit a new copper sealing washer to the drain plug.
  2. Thread the plug in by hand first to avoid cross-threading, then tighten with the torque wrench to 22 Nm (16 ft-lb). Do not overtighten — the crankcase is aluminium and strips easily.

Step 6 — Fill with Fresh Oil

  1. Insert the small funnel into the oil filler neck (accessible from under the open seat).
  2. Pour in 800 ml for the 125 cc engine or 900 ml for the 150 cc engine. Do not use the full workshop capacity of 1,000 ml unless the engine has been fully stripped and the oil filter replaced — overfilling causes foaming and oil consumption through the breather.
  3. Wait 1 minute for oil to settle, then check the level on the dipstick: oil should sit between the MIN and MAX marks, ideally just below MAX.
  4. Refit the filler cap and tighten clockwise until snug. Do not use tools.

Step 7 — Run and Check for Leaks

  1. Start the engine and let it idle for 2–3 minutes.
  2. The oil pressure warning light (if present) should extinguish within 3–5 seconds of starting.
  3. Shut off and inspect the drain plug area for seepage. A correctly torqued plug with a new washer will be dry.
  4. Recheck the dipstick level after shutdown — top up by 50–100 ml if below MIN.

Disposal

Used engine oil must not be poured down drains or disposed of in household waste. Decant it into the empty oil bottle and take it to a local recycling centre, garage, or auto parts shop — most accept used oil free of charge.
