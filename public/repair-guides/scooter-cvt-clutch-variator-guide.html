CVT Clutch, Variator and Roller Inspection and Replacement Guide

Added: 2026-05-08  |  Sources: wemoto.com, louis-moto.com  |  GY6 Piaggio Vespa CVT
📌 Based on: Guide to Scooter Clutches, Variators and Rollers — Wemoto and Scooter Variator and Clutch — Louis Moto Magazine. Also see the ISRM GY6 CVT Variator and Drive Belt Replacement guide for full disassembly procedure.

The continuously variable transmission (CVT) is the automatic gearbox found on virtually all modern petrol scooters — both GY6-platform Chinese machines and Piaggio/Vespa models. It consists of two pulleys connected by a rubber drive belt, with the engine-side pulley (the variator) automatically adjusting its effective diameter based on engine speed. The result is a smooth, stepless gear ratio change without the rider needing to do anything.

CVT components wear gradually and silently. Many riders only notice problems when performance has already significantly degraded. Routine inspection every 6,000–10,000 km will prevent most CVT-related failures.

How the GY6 / Piaggio CVT Works

At low engine speed (idle, pulling away), the variator ramps push the rollers inward — the front pulley is narrow, the belt runs low, giving a low gear ratio for easy acceleration. As engine speed rises, centrifugal force pushes the roller weights outward along the ramps, widening the front pulley and raising the gear ratio until the belt is running at the top of the pulley — effectively "top gear". The rear clutch pulley (torque server / driven pulley) has a spring that provides resistance, ensuring the belt shifts smoothly between ratios.

Key CVT Components

  • Variator (front pulley assembly): Two-piece aluminium pulley with internal roller weights and ramps
  • Roller weights: Small cylindrical or barrel-shaped weights (typically 6 per variator); they move the movable pulley face as engine speed changes
  • Drive belt: Reinforced rubber V-belt; transfers power between front and rear pulley
  • Rear clutch / torque server / driven pulley: Spring-loaded; also contains the centrifugal clutch shoes that engage the rear wheel above a set engine speed
  • Clutch bell (driven housing): The outer drum that the clutch shoes press against to drive the final-reduction gears

Roller Weight Specifications

Roller weights are sold by diameter, length, and mass in grams. Using the correct weight is important: heavier rollers shift up earlier (better fuel economy, lower peak revs at speed); lighter rollers shift up later (more low-down acceleration, higher revving). Stock weights should be used for commuting; lighter weights suit performance builds.

⚠️ Replace rollers as a complete set of six. If even one roller has a flat spot, the whole set is unbalanced. Mixing old and new rollers will not restore smooth operation.

Roller Replacement Procedure (GY6 Platform)

  1. Remove the transmission cover (variator cover) — usually 4–6 bolts on the left side of the engine. Note the gasket or O-ring seal.
  2. To prevent the variator from spinning when loosening the centre nut, insert a strap wrench around the variator face, or use a flywheel holder tool. Do not jam the belt — this can damage it.
  3. Remove the variator centre nut (typically M14 × 1.25, right-hand thread). On most GY6 engines this is torqued to 40–50 Nm.
  4. Slide the outer variator face off the shaft, then lift the belt off the front pulley (it will remain on the rear clutch).
  5. Remove the inner variator face (it may slide or require gentle persuasion). The six roller weights will be visible in the ramp housing. Tip them out.
  6. Inspect ramps for grooves. Light polishing marks are acceptable; deep ridges require variator replacement.
  7. Install six new rollers of the correct weight. Grease the ramp surfaces lightly with high-temperature grease.
  8. Reassemble in reverse order. Torque the variator nut to spec (40–50 Nm GY6; consult your service manual for Piaggio).
  9. Refit the cover with a new gasket if the old one is damaged.

Clutch Shoe Inspection

Clutch shoes are the friction material pads on the rear clutch spider that press against the clutch bell drum. They wear thin over time and must be replaced when the lining thickness falls below approximately 2 mm (check your service manual for the exact limit). Worn shoes cause late clutch engagement (needing higher revs to pull away) and eventually slipping under load.

To inspect: with the rear clutch assembly removed, measure lining thickness with a vernier calliper. Also check the inside of the clutch bell for scoring. Lightly scored bells can be polished with fine emery cloth; deeply grooved bells must be replaced.
