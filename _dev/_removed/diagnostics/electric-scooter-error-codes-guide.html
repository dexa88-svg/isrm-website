Understanding Error Codes

Electric scooters display on-screen error codes to identify faults in critical components. Each code points to a specific system failure—whether in the motor, battery management system (BMS), controller, brakes, or accelerator. Error codes vary between manufacturers; a code on a Ninebot may mean something entirely different on a Dualtron or Xiaomi scooter.

Apollo, Ninebot, and Xiaomi

These manufacturers maintain separate error code tables in their support docs and in-app diagnostics. Always refer to the official manual or support portal for your specific model.

How to Troubleshoot an Error Code

  1. Power cycle: Turn off the scooter, wait 10 seconds, and turn it back on. Some codes clear after a restart.
  2. Check the manual: Look up the error code in your scooter's user manual or manufacturer support site. Brand-specific codes differ.
  3. Visual inspection: Examine cables, connectors, and sensors for damage, loose connections, or corrosion.
  4. Test components: Use a multimeter to check continuity in motor cables, brake sensors, and throttle circuits.
  5. Log the code: If it persists, note the exact code, when it appears, and any patterns (e.g., after rain, during acceleration).
  6. Seek support: Contact the manufacturer with the error code and scooter model. Most brands offer free diagnostics via email or phone.

Codes That Require Professional Help

Motor codes (E1, E001), BMS codes (E6, E006), and repeated electrical errors should be handled by a technician. These indicate issues with sealed components or complex electronics that are risky to repair without training.

Preventative Measures

  • Keep the scooter clean and dry; cover connectors in damp weather.
  • Avoid rapid acceleration or high ambient heat that can trigger overheating codes.
  • Charge regularly to prevent battery under-voltage issues.
  • Check brakes and throttle response weekly; replace worn sensors before they fail.
